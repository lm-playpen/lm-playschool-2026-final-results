\texttt{**Reasoning:**} \\
\texttt{This scenario is a classic example from research on causal attribution (specifically the "double prevention" or "overdetermination" problem). While the design studio employees did physically arrive and their arrival was one of the sufficient conditions for the system to turn on, a typical person is unlikely to attribute the cause of the event *specifically* to the design studio agents because their action was redundant.} \\
\\ 
\texttt{Since the travel agency employees also arrived and were sufficient to trigger the system on their own, the actual turning on of the climate control system was already guaranteed by the travel agency's arrival. From a causal perspective, the design studio's arrival made no difference to the outcome; the outcome would have happened regardless of whether they were there. Therefore, a typical person perceives the cause as the first sufficient condition that actually occurred (the travel agency), denying that the second sufficient condition (the design studio) caused the event.} \\
