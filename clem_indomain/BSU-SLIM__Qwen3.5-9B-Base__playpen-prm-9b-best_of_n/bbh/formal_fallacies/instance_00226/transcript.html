Q: "Here comes a perfectly valid argument: First of all, nobody is neither a classmate of Marisa nor a schoolmate of Karen. Next, not every sister of Ruth is a schoolmate of Karen. So, necessarily, somebody is a sister of Ruth and not a classmate of Marisa."
Is the argument, given the explicitly stated premises, deductively valid or invalid?
Options:
- valid
- invalid
A:

To determine whether the argument is deductively valid or invalid, let's break down the logical structure of the premises and the conclusion.

**1. Symbolize the Premises:**

Let's define the following predicates for the individuals involved:
* $C(x)$: $x$ is a classmate of Marisa.
* $S(x)$: $x$ is a schoolmate of Karen.
* $I(x)$: $x$ is a sister of Ruth.

**Premise 1:** "Nobody is neither a classmate of Marisa nor a schoolmate of Karen."
The phrase "neither A nor B" means someone lacks both A and B. The statement says *nobody* has this property.
Logically, this is equivalent to saying: It is not the case that ($x$ is not a classmate AND $x$ is not a schoolmate).
Formal translation: $\neg \forall x (\neg C(x) \land \neg S(x))$
Equivalently, by De Morgan's laws and quantifier negation: $\forall x \neg (\neg C(x) \land \neg S(x))$, which simplifies to $\forall x (C(x) \lor S(x))$.
*Meaning:* Everyone is either a classmate of Marisa or a schoolmate of Karen (or both).

**Premise 2:** "Not every sister of Ruth is a schoolmate of Karen."
This means there exists at least one sister of Ruth who is *not* a schoolmate of Karen.
Formal translation: $\exists x (I(x) \land \neg S(x))$.
*Meaning:* There is at least one person who is a sister of Ruth and is not a schoolmate of Karen.

**Conclusion:** "Somebody is a sister of Ruth and not a classmate of Marisa."
Formal translation: $\exists x (I(x) \land \neg C(x))$.

**2. Analyze the Deductive Logic:**

* From **Premise 1**, we know that for every person $x$, they must satisfy $C(x) \lor S(x)$.
* From **Premise 2**, we know there exists a specific person, let's call her $a$, such that $I(a)$ is true and $S(a)$ is false ($\neg S(a)$).
* Now, apply Premise 1 to this specific person $a$:
* Since $S(a)$ is false, for the disjunction $C(a) \lor S(a)$ to be true (which it must be, per Premise 1), the other part of the disjunction, $C(a)$, **must** be true.
* Therefore, $C(a)$ is true.
* Since we already know $I(a)$ is true (from Premise 2) and we have just deduced that $C(a)$ is true, it follows that $\neg C(a)$ is **false**.
* The conclusion claims that there exists someone who is a sister ($I(x)$) AND not a classmate ($\neg C(x)$).
* However, for the specific individual $a$ (who is a sister but not a schoolmate), we have proven that $C(a)$ is true. Therefore, the condition "not a classmate of Marisa" ($\neg C(a)$) is false for this person.

**3. Final Determination:**

The premises logically force the individual identified in Premise 2 to *be* a classmate of Marisa. Therefore, the conclusion that this individual is *not* a classmate contradicts the necessary condition established in Premise 1. The argument does not follow logically from the premises.

Since the conclusion does not necessarily follow from the premises, the argument is **invalid**.
