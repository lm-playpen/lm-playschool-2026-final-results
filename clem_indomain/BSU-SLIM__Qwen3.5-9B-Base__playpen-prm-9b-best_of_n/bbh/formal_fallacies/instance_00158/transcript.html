Moreover, every close friend of Francine is a cousin of Samantha. Finally, being a close friend of Shanika is necessary for being a close friend of Francine. We may conclude: Being an ancestor of Janice is necessary for being a close friend of Francine."
Is the argument, given the explicitly stated premises, deductively valid or invalid?

- invalid
A:

To determine whether this argument is **deductively valid** or **invalid**, let's break down the premises and the conclusion using formal logic.

### 1. Formalize the Premises

Let's define the following propositions based on the text:
* $F(x)$: $x$ is a close friend of Francine.
* $C(x)$: $x$ is a cousin of Samantha.
* $A(x)$: $x$ is an ancestor of Janice.
* $S(x)$: $x$ is a close friend of Shanika.

**Premise 1:** "Every close friend of Francine is a cousin of Samantha or an ancestor of Janice or a close friend of Shanika."
* Logical form: $\forall x (F(x) \rightarrow (C(x) \lor A(x) \lor S(x)))$

**Premise 2:** "Every close friend of Francine is a cousin of Samantha."
* Logical form: $\forall x (F(x) \rightarrow C(x))$

**Premise 3:** "Being a close friend of Shanika is necessary for being a close friend of Francine."
* Recall that "P is necessary for Q" translates to $Q \rightarrow P$.
* Logical form: $F(x) \rightarrow S(x)$

**Conclusion:** "Being an ancestor of Janice is necessary for being a close friend of Francine."
* Again, "P is necessary for Q" translates to $Q \rightarrow P$. Here, $P$ is $A(x)$ (ancestor) and $Q$ is $F(x)$ (friend).
* Logical form: $F(x) \rightarrow A(x)$

### 2. Logical Analysis

We have the following set of implications for any arbitrary $x$:
1. $F(x) \rightarrow (C(x) \lor A(x) \lor S(x))$
2. $F(x) \rightarrow C(x)$
3. $F(x) \rightarrow S(x)$

The conclusion claims:
* $F(x) \rightarrow A(x)$

**Deductive Validity Check:**
An argument is valid if, assuming the premises are true, the conclusion *must* be true.

Let's test the logic:
* From Premise 2, we know that if anyone is a close friend of Francine ($F(x)$), they are definitely a cousin of Samantha ($C(x)$).
* From Premise 3, we know that if anyone is a close friend of Francine ($F(x)$), they are definitely a close friend of Shanika ($S(x)$).
* Premise 1 states that if $F(x)$ is true, then at least one of the conditions ($C$, $A$, or $S$) must be true.

Since Premises 2 and 3 are stronger than Premise 1 (they assert specific conditions are true, whereas Premise 1 only asserts that *at least one* of the conditions is true), the conclusion that $F(x) \rightarrow A(x)$ does **not** follow.

In fact, based on the premises provided:
* We know $F(x) \rightarrow C(x)$ is true.
* We know $F(x) \rightarrow S(x)$ is true.
* We do **not** know that $F(x) \rightarrow A(x)$ is true. Someone could be a close friend of Francine, be a cousin of Samantha, and be a friend of Shanika, but *not* be an ancestor of Janice. The disjunction in Premise 1 is satisfied by $C(x)$ or $S(x)$, leaving $A(x)$ potentially false.

Therefore, the truth of the premises does not guarantee the truth of the conclusion. There is a logical gap; the argument commits a formal fallacy (specifically, a fallacy of denying the antecedent or confusing a disjunction with a conjunction regarding the necessary conditions).

Since the conclusion does not necessarily follow from the premises, the argument is **invalid**.
