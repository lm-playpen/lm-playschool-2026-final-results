\texttt{2.  **Premise 2**: Whoever is not both a classmate of Hazel and a cousin of Annie is a great{-}grandmother of Doria.} \\
\texttt{    *   Let \$P(x)\$ represent "\$x\$ is a classmate of Hazel".} \\
\texttt{    *   Let \$Q(x)\$ represent "\$x\$ is a cousin of Annie".} \\
\texttt{    *   Let \$R(x)\$ represent "\$x\$ is a great{-}grandmother of Doria".} \\
\texttt{    *   The logical form is: \$\textbackslash{}neg(P(x) \textbackslash{}land Q(x)) \textbackslash{}implies R(x)\$.} \\
\\ 
\texttt{3.  **Conclusion**: It is false that Merle is a great{-}grandmother of Doria.} \\
\texttt{    *   The logical form is: \$\textbackslash{}neg R(\textbackslash{}text\{Merle\})\$.} \\
\\ 
\texttt{**Logical Analysis:**} \\
\texttt{*   From Premise 1, we know that for Merle, the condition "\$x\$ is a cousin of Annie" (\$Q(\textbackslash{}text\{Merle\})\$) is **True**.} \\
\texttt{*   However, Premise 2 establishes a condition for being a great{-}grandmother based on the conjunction: "not both a classmate of Hazel **and** a cousin of Annie" (\$\textbackslash{}neg(P \textbackslash{}land Q)\$).} \\
\texttt{*   For the implication \$\textbackslash{}neg(P \textbackslash{}land Q) \textbackslash{}implies R\$ to trigger (i.e., for the consequent \$R\$ to be true), the antecedent \$\textbackslash{}neg(P \textbackslash{}land Q)\$ must be true. This requires \$P \textbackslash{}land Q\$ to be false.} \\
\texttt{*   Since \$Q(\textbackslash{}text\{Merle\})\$ is **True**, the conjunction \$(P \textbackslash{}land \textbackslash{}text\{True\})\$ has the same truth value as \$P\$. Therefore, the antecedent is \$\textbackslash{}neg P\$.} \\
\texttt{*   The premises do **not** state that Merle is *not* a classmate of Hazel. Merle could be a classmate of Hazel (\$P\$ is True), which would make the conjunction \$(P \textbackslash{}land Q)\$ True, and consequently \$\textbackslash{}neg(P \textbackslash{}land Q)\$ False.} \\
\texttt{*   In classical logic, if the antecedent is False, the implication is vacuously true, but it tells us nothing about the truth value of the consequent (\$R\$). Therefore, we cannot deduce that \$R(\textbackslash{}text\{Merle\})\$ is false based solely on these premises.
