Q: "Here comes a perfectly valid argument: First, everyone who is a half-brother of Ronny or a classmate of Sal is not a son of Ronald. Second, it is false that Santo is a son of Ronald. So, necessarily, Santo is a half-brother of Ronny or a classmate of Sal."
Is the argument, given the explicitly stated premises, deductively valid or invalid?
Options:
- valid
- invalid
A:

This argument is **invalid**.

Here is the step-by-step logical breakdown:

1. **Analyze the Premises:**
* **Premise 1:** $(H \lor C) \rightarrow \neg S$
* (If someone is a half-brother of Ronny OR a classmate of Sal, THEN they are NOT a son of Ronald).
* This establishes a sufficient condition: Being a half-brother or classmate guarantees that the person is *not* a son.
* **Premise 2:** $\neg S$ is true for Santo.
* (Santo is NOT a son of Ronald).

2. **Analyze the Conclusion:**
* **Conclusion:** $H \lor C$ is true for Santo.
* (Santo is a half-brother of Ronny OR a classmate of Sal).

3. **Identify the Logical Error:**
* The argument attempts to use the fallacy of the **fallacy of the inverse** (or confusing the inverse with the converse).
* From Premise 1, we know that if the antecedent ($H \lor C$) is true, then the negation of the consequent ($\neg S$) must be true.
* However, simply knowing that the *consequent* ($\neg S$) is true does **not** prove that the *antecedent* ($H \lor C$) is true.
* **Counter-example:** Santo might be neither a half-brother nor a classmate. In that case, Santo is still not a son of Ronald (perhaps Santo is an estranged brother, or an adopted child, or just a random person who happened not to be a son). The premises do not rule out other reasons why someone might not be a son of Ronald.

4. **Formal Logic Check:**
* Let $P$ = is half-brother or classmate.
* Let $Q$ = is son.
* Premise: $P \rightarrow \neg Q$
* Premise: $\neg Q$ (for Santo)
* Conclusion: $P$
* The structure is $P \rightarrow \neg Q$ and $\neg Q$, therefore $P$. This is a classic invalid form ($Denying the Antecedent$ if phrased differently, or affirming the Consequent in the contrapositive sense). Specifically, it is invalid because the truth of the consequent does not guarantee the truth of the antecedent.
